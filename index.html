Working Papers

"Does Eviction Break or Perpetuate Domestic Violence Cycles? Gender-Heterogeneous DV Dynamics Around Eviction in Wisconsin Court Records" Job Market Paper
Single-authored
Links 163,000 eviction filings, 57,000 DV criminal cases, and 26,000 restraining orders from Wisconsin courts to 13.2 million person-years of address histories. Introduces a novel address-based DV exposure measure to show that both male abusers and female survivors experience DV declines post-eviction through different channels: partner separation and residential displacement, respectively.
"The AI Housing Advisor: Information Democratization or Algorithmic Discrimination?"
Single-authored
Studies whether LLMs expand or distort housing information access for vulnerable populations. Combines a multi-model audit experiment (500K+ prompts, 37K household personas, 4 LLMs), counterfactual benchmarking against non-AI information sources, and observational panel evidence from the Bay Area to provide the first net welfare estimate of AI-mediated housing search across race, income, education, and language proficiency groups.
"Court Record and Tenant Screening Algorithm, and Housing Discrimination"
With Molly Clark Barol and Christopher Timmins. Working Paper, 2026.
Links Wisconsin's public court records (CCAP) to consumer data and shows that individuals sharing names with court-record holders are sorted into lower-rent, higher-poverty neighborhoods — consistent with algorithmic tenant screening penalizing common-name individuals. Confirms the mechanism using Levenshtein name-distance fuzzy matching.
"Waste Material Imports and the Production-Pollution Tradeoff: Evidence and Policy"
With Ivan Cai and Luyi Zeng
Studies how waste material imports affect firm-level pollution and productivity in China from 2000–2013. Using a shift-share IV strategy, finds a clear production-pollution tradeoff and develops a general equilibrium model showing that optimal policy requires both environmental taxes and waste import tariffs.
"Retail Gentrification: Environmental Improvements and Residential Sorting — Evidence from Brownfield Cleanup"
Single-authored
Examines whether place-based environmental cleanup programs lead to displacement of vulnerable populations through retail gentrification. Using household-level longitudinal data from Cook County, IL (2010–2019), combines Illinois EPA brownfield remediation records, the InfoUSA household panel, and the NETS retail database within a Callaway & Sant'Anna difference-in-differences and propensity-score-matched probit framework. Finds that brownfield cleanup initially reduces out-migration, but subsequent retail amenity growth significantly increases displacement of minority renters to more polluted neighborhoods.
